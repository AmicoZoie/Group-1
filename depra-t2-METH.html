Methodology

1. Site Selection & Classification

•The researchers selected seven hawker stall locations along the East Coast of Peninsular Malaysia (in Terengganu state).

•Sites were categorized into three environmental types: suburban, urban, and industrial.

•These classifications were used to compare how microplastic deposition varied with surrounding land use and pollution sources.

2. Sampling of Airborne Microplastics

•Dry deposition sampling was used — this captures microplastic particles that settle out of the air onto surfaces over time.

•Whatman glass fiber filter paper (0.2 µm pore size) was deployed in the open-air environment at each site.

•These filters collected particles over a fixed period (details such as duration generally specified in the full article).

3. Laboratory Processing

•Once collected, samples underwent several analytical procedures:

a. Sorting & Counting

•Particles trapped on filters were manually separated and counted under a digital stereomicroscope to determine abundance (items per square meter per day).

b. Morphological Characterization

•The physical features of microplastics were recorded, including shape (e.g., fibers vs. fragments), size, and color distribution.

c. Polymer Identification

•ATR-FTIR (Attenuated Total Reflectance Fourier-Transform Infrared Spectroscopy) was used to chemically confirm what polymers the microplastics were made of (e.g., polyester, nylon).

4. Data Analysis

•Deposition fluxes were calculated (e.g., number of particles per m² per day).

•The researchers compared microplastic abundances, shapes, colors, and polymer identities across the three different site types (suburban vs. urban vs. industrial).

•Statistical analysis was used to determine significant differences in deposition patterns among these environments.
